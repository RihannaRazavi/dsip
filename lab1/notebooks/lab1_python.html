Introduction

Understanding how environmental conditions vary in forest canopies is essential for predicting tree physiology, carbon cycling, and ecosystem responses to climate change. Temperature and humidity are fundamental drivers of photosynthesis and transpiration in trees, yet their interactions within tall forest canopies remain poorly understood due to the difficulty of measuring these variables at multiple heights all together.

Coastal redwood (Sequoia sempervirens) trees create complex microclimates due to their extreme height (often exceeding 70 meters) and dense canopy structure. Traditional ecological theory suggests that upper canopy positions should be hotter and drier due to direct solar exposure, while lower canopy positions should be cooler and more humid. However, the actual temperature-humidity relationships at different canopy heights, and how these relationships reflect different environmental processes, have rarely been quantified at fine spatial scales.

Research Question: How does the relationship between temperature and humidity vary across different vertical positions within a coastal redwood canopy?

This analysis uses high-resolution sensor network data from Tolle et al. (2005) collected over 44 days in a single redwood tree to examine how temperature-humidity relationships differ between lower, middle, and upper canopy positions.

Data Quality Issues and Cleaning Procedures

Through systematic examination, we identified several critical data quality issues:

1. Voltage Scale Inconsistency

The network and log datasets recorded voltage in fundamentally different scales. The network dataset used raw ADC (analog-to-digital converter) values ranging from 0-1023, while the log dataset stored actual voltages in the 0-3.3V range. Simply merging these datasets would create artificial patterns and invalidate any voltage-based quality filtering.

Rather than applying a theoretical conversion formula (which assumes knowledge of the exact reference voltage and ADC configuration),I used an empirical calibration approach. We matched the statistical distributions of the two datasets by finding the linear transformation (scale factor and offset) that aligns their percentiles. V_corrected = 0.016291 × V_net + -0.926464

2. Invalid Voltage Readings

We filtered data to retain only readings with voltage between 0-4V. While the typical operating range for these sensors is 2.4-3.0V (shown as dotted green/blue lines in the plots). Values outside 0-4V represent sensor malfunctions or data corruption.

3. Impossible Physical Values

We removed readings that violated physical constraints: - Humidity outside 0-100% (relative humidity cannot exceed saturation or be negative) - Temperature outside -20°C to 50°C (far outside the range possible in coastal California) - Negative PAR values (light intensity cannot be negative)

4. Duplicate Measurements

After merging the network and log datasets, some measurements appeared twice. We removed duplicates by identifying measurements with matching sensor ID, timestamp, and measurement period, keeping only the first occurrence.

def plot_temp_humidity_by_layer(data, figsize=(16, 5)):
    """
    Plot temperature vs humidity relationship for different canopy layers
    """
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=figsize)
    
    colors = {'Lower': 'forestgreen', 'Middle': 'orange', 'Upper': 'crimson'}
    
    for i, layer in enumerate(['Lower', 'Middle', 'Upper']):
        subset = data[data['canopy_layer'] == layer]
        
        axes[i].hexbin(subset['humid_temp'], subset['humidity'], 
                       gridsize=30, cmap='YlOrRd', mincnt=1, alpha=0.8)
        axes[i].set_xlabel('Temperature (°C)', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
        axes[i].set_ylabel('Relative Humidity (%)', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
        axes[i].set_title(f'{layer} Canopy', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold', 
                         color=colors[layer])
        axes[i].grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        n = len(subset)
        axes[i].text(0.05, 0.95, f'n = {n:,}', transform=axes[i].transAxes,
                    fontsize=10, verticalalignment='top',
                    bbox=dict(boxstyle='round', facecolor='wheat', alpha=0.5))
    
    plt.tight_layout()
    return fig

def plot_humidity_violins(data, figsize=(8, 6)):
    """
    Violin plot of humidity by canancy layer
    """
    import matplotlib.pyplot as plt  # Move import to TOP
    
    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=figsize)
    
    layers = ['Lower', 'Middle', 'Upper']
    data_to_plot = [data[data['canopy_layer'] == layer]['humidity'].dropna().values 
                    for layer in layers]
    colors = ['forestgreen', 'orange', 'pink']
    
    parts = ax.violinplot(data_to_plot, positions=[1, 2, 3], 
                          showmeans=True, showmedians=True, widths=0.7)
    
    for i, pc in enumerate(parts['bodies']):
        pc.set_facecolor(colors[i])
        pc.set_alpha(0.5)
    
    # Color the lines
    for partname in ('cbars', 'cmins', 'cmaxes', 'cmedians', 'cmeans'):
        if partname in parts:
            parts[partname].set_edgecolor('black')
            parts[partname].set_linewidth(1.5)
    
    ax.set_xticks([1, 2, 3])
    ax.set_xticklabels(layers)
    ax.set_xlabel('Canopy Layer', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
    ax.set_ylabel('Relative Humidity (%)', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
    ax.set_title('Humidity Distribution by Canopy Layer', 
                 fontsize=15, fontweight='bold')
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3, axis='y')
    
    plt.tight_layout()
    return fig

fig = plot_humidity_violins(analysis_data)
plt.show()

Median humidity values are similar across layers, approximately 55–60%, indicating that typical humidity is consistent throughout the canopy. However, the distribution shapes reveal layer-specific variability. The Lower and Middle layers exhibit wider spreads at low humidity values, suggesting that these layers experience more frequent dry conditions. In contrast, the Upper layer shows a slightly narrower distribution around the median, with fewer extremely low values, though it still reaches high humidity levels. Overall, while central tendencies are similar, the lower and middle canopy layers are subject to greater fluctuations in relative humidity, likely reflecting microclimatic effects such as differential sunlight exposure and airflow.

Discussion

This analysis demonstrates that temperature-humidity relationships in redwood canopies vary systematically with vertical position, revealing fundamentally different environmental processes operating at different heights. The three key findings tell a coherent story about canopy microclimate structure:

The strength of temperature-humidity coupling increases with height. Upper canopy sensors show tight inverse relationships characteristic of direct atmospheric forcing, while lower canopy sensors show weak, diffuse relationships indicating buffering and decoupling from atmospheric conditions. This gradient in coupling strength reflects the transition from exposed, atmosphere-dominated conditions at the top to buffered, tree-physiology-influenced conditions at the bottom.

Second, despite dramatic differences in light exposure and humidity levels, temperature remains relatively uniform across canopy heights. This thermal uniformity, combined with varying humidity, creates distinct microenvironments: the upper canopy is warm and dry while the lower canopy is warm and humid. The combination of similar temperatures but different humidities means that vapor pressure deficit (the driving force for transpiration) varies substantially with height even though temperature alone would suggest similar conditions.

From a tree physiology perspective, these findings have important implications. The upper canopy operates under high evaporative demand (low humidity) despite moderate temperatures, requiring substantial water transport from roots. The tight temperature-humidity coupling means that upper leaves experience coordinated stress from both factors. The lower canopy benefits from high humidity reducing water stress, and the weak temperature-humidity coupling means that fog events can raise humidity independent of temperature changes, providing additional moisture without requiring temperature drops.

These patterns also highlight the limitations of using temperature alone as a microclimate indicator. Two positions with identical temperatures (as seen in Finding 2) can have dramatically different humidity levels and different environmental forcing mechanisms (as seen in Findings 1 and 3). A complete understanding of canopy microclimate requires examining multiple variables and their relationships, not just their individual distributions.

Future work could examine how these temperature-humidity relationships vary diurnally (day versus night), seasonally, or during specific weather events like fog intrusions or heat waves. Comparing multiple trees or forest stands would test whether these patterns are general features of tall forest canopies or specific to this particular redwood tree and its coastal location.

# Introduction

Understanding how environmental conditions vary in forest canopies is essential for predicting tree physiology, carbon cycling, and ecosystem responses to climate change. Temperature and humidity are fundamental drivers of photosynthesis and transpiration in trees, yet their interactions within tall forest canopies remain poorly understood due to the difficulty of measuring these variables at multiple heights all together.

Coastal redwood (*Sequoia sempervirens*) trees create complex microclimates due to their extreme height (often exceeding 70 meters) and dense canopy structure. Traditional ecological theory suggests that upper canopy positions should be hotter and drier due to direct solar exposure, while lower canopy positions should be cooler and more humid. However, the actual temperature-humidity relationships at different canopy heights, and how these relationships reflect different environmental processes, have rarely been quantified at fine spatial scales.

**Research Question:** How does the relationship between temperature and humidity vary across different vertical positions within a coastal redwood canopy? 


This analysis uses high-resolution sensor network data from Tolle et al. (2005) collected over 44 days in a single redwood tree to examine how temperature-humidity relationships differ between lower, middle, and upper canopy positions.

## Data Quality Issues and Cleaning Procedures

Through systematic examination, we identified several critical data quality issues:

### 1. Voltage Scale Inconsistency

The network and log datasets recorded voltage in fundamentally different scales. The network dataset used raw ADC (analog-to-digital converter) values ranging from 0-1023, while the log dataset stored actual voltages in the 0-3.3V range. Simply merging these datasets would create artificial patterns and invalidate any voltage-based quality filtering.

Rather than applying a theoretical conversion formula (which assumes knowledge of the exact reference voltage and ADC configuration),I used an empirical calibration approach. We matched the statistical distributions of the two datasets by finding the linear transformation (scale factor and offset) that aligns their percentiles. 
 V_corrected = 0.016291 × V_net + -0.926464

### 2. Invalid Voltage Readings

We filtered data to retain only readings with voltage between 0-4V. While the typical operating range for these sensors is 2.4-3.0V (shown as dotted green/blue lines in the plots). Values outside 0-4V represent sensor malfunctions or data corruption.

### 3. Impossible Physical Values

We removed readings that violated physical constraints:
- Humidity outside 0-100% (relative humidity cannot exceed saturation or be negative)
- Temperature outside -20°C to 50°C (far outside the range possible in coastal California)
- Negative PAR values (light intensity cannot be negative)

### 4. Duplicate Measurements

After merging the network and log datasets, some measurements appeared twice. We removed duplicates by identifying measurements with matching sensor ID, timestamp, and measurement period, keeping only the first occurrence.

# Discussion

This analysis demonstrates that temperature-humidity relationships in redwood canopies vary systematically with vertical position, revealing fundamentally different environmental processes operating at different heights. The three key findings tell a coherent story about canopy microclimate structure:

The strength of temperature-humidity coupling increases with height. Upper canopy sensors show tight inverse relationships characteristic of direct atmospheric forcing, while lower canopy sensors show weak, diffuse relationships indicating buffering and decoupling from atmospheric conditions. This gradient in coupling strength reflects the transition from exposed, atmosphere-dominated conditions at the top to buffered, tree-physiology-influenced conditions at the bottom.

Second, despite dramatic differences in light exposure and humidity levels, temperature remains relatively uniform across canopy heights. This thermal uniformity, combined with varying humidity, creates distinct microenvironments: the upper canopy is warm and dry while the lower canopy is warm and humid. The combination of similar temperatures but different humidities means that vapor pressure deficit (the driving force for transpiration) varies substantially with height even though temperature alone would suggest similar conditions.


From a tree physiology perspective, these findings have important implications. The upper canopy operates under high evaporative demand (low humidity) despite moderate temperatures, requiring substantial water transport from roots. The tight temperature-humidity coupling means that upper leaves experience coordinated stress from both factors. The lower canopy benefits from high humidity reducing water stress, and the weak temperature-humidity coupling means that fog events can raise humidity independent of temperature changes, providing additional moisture without requiring temperature drops.

These patterns also highlight the limitations of using temperature alone as a microclimate indicator. Two positions with identical temperatures (as seen in Finding 2) can have dramatically different humidity levels and different environmental forcing mechanisms (as seen in Findings 1 and 3). A complete understanding of canopy microclimate requires examining multiple variables and their relationships, not just their individual distributions.

Future work could examine how these temperature-humidity relationships vary diurnally (day versus night), seasonally, or during specific weather events like fog intrusions or heat waves. Comparing multiple trees or forest stands would test whether these patterns are general features of tall forest canopies or specific to this particular redwood tree and its coastal location.
